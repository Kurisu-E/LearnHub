incisive adjective
in·​ci·​sive in-ˈsī-siv
1

of communication or a person : impressively direct and decisive (as in manner or presentation) : able to explain difficult ideas clearly and confidently

an incisive analysis/commentary/observation

an incisive unsentimental writer

She's known for her incisive mind and quick wit.

Synonyms & Similar Words

  • acidic
  • acid
  • keen
incisively adverb

an incisively funny film

He was written incisively about the problem.

incisiveness noun
non-countable

the incisiveness of her analysis

2

medical : incisal

also : of, relating to, or situated near the incisors

Word History

First Known Use

circa 1834

Etymology

Middle French or Latin;

⬅ Middle French (mid 14c. to early 17c.) incisif "slashing, cutting with a sharp edge" ⬅ Old French (9c. to early 14c.) incisif ← Medieval Latin incisivus (12c.) "(medical) invasive, effective" ⇠ Latin incis- (see incision) + -īvus "of or relating to doing";

⬅ or directly Medieval Latin incisivus

Semantic Change

incisive sense 1 : effective in communication (of expression, or, metonymically, one who is able to be) ← Middle French incisif ← Old French incisif ← Medieval Latin incisivus "of or relating to affecting or influencing effectively specially in communication" ⇠ Latin incidere/incis- "to affect or influence effectively" + -īvus "of or relating to doing" ← Latin incidere "to carve or engrave metaphorically into (something, such as minds, ideas, actions, or emotions)" ⬅ meiotically to "to carve or engrave inward (something, such as a wood)" ⬅ Latin incidere "to cut inward (something, such as a wood)" ⇠ Latin in- "inward, in, into" + -cidere/caedere "to cut"

incisive sense 2 : invasive specially in density ⬅ Old French incisif "(medical) invasive", incision "a cutting made in surgery" ← Medieval Latin incisivus "of or relating to cutting or incising specially in surgery" ⇠ Latin incidere/incis- "to cut or incise" + -īvus "of or relating to doing" ← Latin incidere "to cut inward (something, such as a wood)"
